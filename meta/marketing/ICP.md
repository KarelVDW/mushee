# Solkey — Ideal Customer Profiles

Working document for marketing. Grounded in what the product actually does today:
a browser-based sheet-music editor whose differentiator is **live audio-to-notation**
(play or sing → notation appears, then fix it with keystrokes and hear it back),
with autosave, mobile support, and pricing of Free "Sketch" (30 s recording/day),
Composer $8/mo (10 min/day), Studio $18/mo (unlimited).

The strategic thread across all three ICPs: **people for whom notation is a
bottleneck between "music in my head/hands" and "music on paper someone else can
read".** Solkey removes the slowest step (note entry) — every ICP below feels that
pain differently.

---

## ICP 1 — "The Idea-Catcher": self-taught songwriter & by-ear musician

**One-liner:** Writes music by ear, can barely read notation, and needs to get
ideas out of their head into a shareable, editable form before the idea dies.

### Snapshot
- **Age:** 18–35 (core 20–29)
- **Occupation:** student, early-career job, or gigging semi-pro; music is a serious
  hobby or side income
- **Instruments:** guitar, piano/keys, voice — often "campfire-grade" theory,
  strong ears
- **Geography:** US/UK/EU/LATAM, English-content consumers
- **Budget mindset:** pays for Spotify and maybe one plugin/app subscription;
  $8/mo is an easy yes *if* the tool is used weekly

### Jobs to be done
1. Capture a melody/riff *right now* (voice memo graveyard problem) — but as
   something structured, not a dead audio file.
2. Turn a song idea into a **lead sheet or part** a bandmate, worship team, or
   session player can actually read.
3. Tweak the idea (key, rhythm, harmony) and *hear it back* without learning a DAW
   or notation software.

### Current tools & pains
- Voice memos + forgotten ideas; Ultimate Guitar chord sheets; maybe GarageBand.
- Tried MuseScore once, bounced off manual note entry ("I don't think in noteheads").
- AI transcription apps (Moises, Klangio) give them stems or MIDI, not an editable
  score in the same place they can fix mistakes.

### Trigger moments
- Band/worship team asks "can you send me the chart?"
- Wants to copyright/document an original song.
- Saw a creator hum into a phone and get sheet music out (this is *the* viral demo
  for this ICP).

### Why Solkey wins
- Hum/play → notation is the whole pitch; the editor is the safety net for the
  transcription's mistakes, not a separate skill to learn.
- Browser + phone: works where the idea happens.
- Free tier's 30 s/day is exactly one idea — perfect wedge, natural upgrade
  pressure for "daily writers" (Composer's literal tagline).

### Objections / risks
- "The transcription got my rhythm wrong" → onboarding must show the fix-a-note
  keystroke flow immediately; accuracy expectations need managing.
- Weak notation literacy means the *output* must also be exportable as something
  they can share (PDF/MusicXML) without understanding it deeply.
- Churn risk if songwriting is seasonal — win-back campaigns matter.

### Marketing channels
- YouTube/TikTok/Instagram songwriting & musician creators (see INFLUENCERS.md,
  niche B); short-form "hum → sheet music in 20 seconds" demos.
- r/Songwriting, r/WeAreTheMusicMakers, worship-leader communities.

**Tier mapping:** Free → Composer. Studio only for heavy co-writers/arrangers.

## ICP 2 — "The Notation-Native Student": music students & serious adult learners

**One-liner:** Studies music formally (or self-studies seriously), regularly has to
*produce* notation — theory homework, arrangements, transcriptions — and hates that
note entry takes longer than the musical thinking.

### Snapshot
- **Age:** two clusters — 15–24 (high school through conservatory/college) and
  30–55 (adult returners taking exams/lessons seriously)
- **Occupation:** student (music major/minor, AP/ABRSM/RCM candidates) or
  professional-elsewhere adult learner
- **Instruments:** piano, strings, winds, voice — reads notation comfortably
- **Budget mindset:** students are price-anchored to free (MuseScore); adults will
  pay for anything that saves practice/homework time

### Jobs to be done
1. Complete theory/harmony/arranging assignments faster than pen-and-paper or
   click-by-click entry.
2. **Transcribe** — jazz solos, ear-training exercises, pieces with no available
   sheet music — by playing back what they hear, phrase by phrase.
3. Check their own playing: record a passage, *see* what they actually played vs.
   what the page says.
4. Make practice materials: transposed excerpts, simplified passages, scale sheets.

### Current tools & pains
- MuseScore (free, powerful, desktop-bound, slow entry), Flat/Noteflight via school
  accounts (browser but no serious audio input), Sibelius/Dorico if the school pays.
- Transcription today = loop audio at half speed + manually enter notes. Solkey
  collapses that loop: play the phrase on your instrument → notation.

### Trigger moments
- Start of semester / new theory course; jazz program transcription assignments;
  exam prep season (ABRSM/RCM/AP).
- A teacher or classmate demos it.

### Why Solkey wins
- Play-in entry is *faster than typing* for people who already play fluently —
  this crowd feels that speed difference most.
- Zero install on school Chromebooks/lab machines; autosave means no lost homework.
- 30 s/day free is a real trial; 10 min/day at $8 covers a homework session.

### Objections / risks
- "MuseScore is free" — must win on speed-of-entry and the record-yourself loop,
  not feature count. Don't fight Dorico on engraving depth.
- Needs solid MusicXML export so it slots into a school's existing toolchain.
- Student pricing/discounts will eventually be expected.

### Marketing channels
- **Primary bet:** YouTube music-education/theory channels (see INFLUENCERS.md,
  niche A) — sponsorships and "I transcribed X by playing it" formats.
- r/musictheory, r/piano, r/Jazz; college music-department Discords.

**Tier mapping:** Free (light users) → Composer. Volume opportunity later:
school/site licences — but that's a different motion, don't build for it yet.

## ICP 3 — "The Studio Teacher": independent music teachers & school music educators

**One-liner:** Teaches 15–60 students a week, constantly produces custom exercises
and simplified arrangements, and recommends tools that their entire studio then
adopts.

### Snapshot
- **Age:** 28–60
- **Occupation:** private studio teacher (piano, guitar, voice, strings), school
  music teacher, band/choir director; often self-employed
- **Musical profile:** conservatory-trained or long-time professional; notation
  fluent, time poor
- **Budget mindset:** already pays for business tools (My Music Staff, Tonara,
  Zoom); $8–18/mo is a business expense, not a hobby purchase. Lowest churn of the
  three ICPs.

### Jobs to be done
1. Produce lesson materials fast: a simplified left hand, a transposed duet part,
   a sight-reading exercise at exactly the right level.
2. Capture what a student (or they themselves) just played and show it as notation —
   "here's what you played vs. what's written" is a powerful teaching moment.
3. Arrange for their specific ensemble (unbalanced school bands, mixed-level
   groups) without wrestling desktop software.
4. Give students something to use at home that doesn't require installing anything.

### Current tools & pains
- Sibelius/Finale muscle memory (expensive, desktop), MuseScore, pencil + staff
  paper for anything quick. Finale's 2024 discontinuation left many teachers
  actively shopping for a new home.
- Existing browser tools (Flat, Noteflight) are classroom-friendly but weak on
  audio input — Solkey's record-to-notation is a genuinely new teaching tool here.

### Trigger moments
- Back-to-school prep (Aug–Sep) and recital season; Finale refugees migrating;
  a colleague's recommendation in a teacher community (this niche is *very*
  word-of-mouth dense).

### Why Solkey wins
- The record-your-student loop doesn't exist in their current stack.
- Browser-based = works on the studio iPad, the school lab, and the student's
  Chromebook identically.
- Each converted teacher is a distribution node: they expose 15–60 students to the
  product per week.

### Objections / risks
- Professional engraving expectations (layout, parts extraction) exceed what a
  young editor offers — position as the *fast lane* next to their heavyweight tool,
  not a replacement, until the editor matures.
- Schools ask about privacy/COPPA/DPAs for under-13 students — a real gate for the
  classroom segment; private studio teachers are the nearer-term entry.
- They will find every notation bug. That's a feature (feedback) but demands
  responsive support — Studio tier's "priority support" fits.

### Marketing channels
- Music-teacher pedagogy channels, podcasts, and communities (see INFLUENCERS.md,
  niche C); teacher Facebook groups; MTNA/ISM-style associations later.

**Tier mapping:** Composer → Studio (unlimited recording fits lesson-length use).
Highest LTV of the three.

## Prioritization & how the ICPs interact

| | ICP 1 Idea-Catcher | ICP 2 Student | ICP 3 Teacher |
|---|---|---|---|
| Market size | Largest | Large | Smallest, densest |
| Willingness to pay | Medium | Low–medium | High |
| Churn risk | High | Medium (semester cycles) | Low |
| Virality | High (short-form demos) | Medium | High (1 teacher → many students) |
| Product fit today | Strong (capture) | Strong (speed of entry) | Partial (engraving gaps) |
| Beta-feedback value | Medium | High | Highest |

- **Lead with ICP 2 for content marketing** (the user-stated primary: music-ed
  YouTube) — the audience overlaps heavily with ICP 1, and educators lend
  credibility that pure product demos don't.
- **ICP 1 is the volume play** for short-form/viral content; same demo assets,
  different framing.
- **ICP 3 is the compounding play**: slowest to convert, highest LTV, and each
  conversion markets to students automatically. Start with private studio teachers;
  defer school/classroom (procurement + child-privacy requirements).
